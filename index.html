About

Bioinformatics with biological context

From life science to computational biology

My academic background began in life science engineering, where I built a foundation in molecular biology, biotechnology, chemistry, mathematics, and data analysis. Over time, I became increasingly drawn to the questions that appear when biological systems are measured at scale: what patterns are hidden in the data, how do they relate to disease, and when can they tell us something biologically meaningful?

What drives me

I am an MSc Eng in Bioinformatics and Systems Biology with a strong interest in cancer omics, survival analysis, and the interpretation of complex molecular data. I am especially interested in work where transcriptomics, proteomics, isoform-level signals, mutations, and clinical outcomes need to be considered together.

I am drawn to problems that are initially messy: many data layers, many possible explanations, and no obvious structure. I enjoy turning that complexity into reproducible analyses, clear visualisations, and coherent scientific stories — without losing the curiosity that made the question interesting in the first place.

For me, good bioinformatics is not only about finding patterns in data, but about understanding what those patterns may mean in a biological context.

MSc thesis · Multi-omics · Survival analysis

Master thesis

My master thesis investigates how isoform-level expression, mutation-level features, transcriptomics, proteomics, and clinical metadata contribute to cancer survival. The project involved aligning molecular data with clinical metadata, building reproducible computational workflows, and applying Cox proportional hazards modelling to explore patient-level outcomes across molecular data layers. Beyond identifying survival-associated features, the project contributes a structured framework for understanding whether signals are shared across omics layers, specific to individual representations, or shaped by isoform- and mutation-level biology.
